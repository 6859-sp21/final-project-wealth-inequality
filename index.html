Understanding a Simple Model for Wealth Distribution: Market Capitalism Leads to High Inequalities

An interactive simulation-based approach to policies for wealth inequality.

Introduction

In a 2013 speech for the Center for American Progress, former President Barack Obama described rising inequality as the "defining challenge of our time." Despite his administration’s promise to focus their efforts on addressing inequalities, the United States continues to be one of the most unequal of the developed nations, surpassed only by Chile, Mexico, and Turkey. In fact, wealth inequality appears to be rising in most developing nations, including those with a model of welfare-state capitalism, such as countries in continental Europe.

Proponents of market capitalism argue that minimal government intervention results in a fair distribution of wealth, with market prices in equilibrium with supply and demand. Milton Friedman, the father of modern libertarian thought, believed that a free market could organize and distribute resources efficiently. Any remaining inequalities are the product of just disparity, where every individual owns the amount of wealth that they have earned. By this argument, the poor are trapped in poverty because of their lack of productivity rather than any structural unfairness in the system.

On the other hand, in his landmark 2014 work Capital in the 21st Century, French economist Thomas Piketty argues that capitalism has an inherent tendency to create very high degrees of wealth and income inequality because in capitalist economies, the rate of return on capital (\(r\)) is greater than the general growth rate (\(g\)). Because of this “\(r>g\)” dynamic, we can expect capitalism, unchecked, to hand most economic and political power to the richest classes, and the ultra-wealthy in particular. This is true even for welfare-state capitalism. The welfare state merely provides social insurance; it has no built-in mechanism for compressing the inequalities of wealth required to dampen the power of \(r>g\).

Despite seemingly opposite arguments on wealth inequality which are often correlated with political ideology, a 2011 study found that U.S. citizens across the political spectrum dramatically underestimate the current level of wealth inequality in the U.S., and would prefer a far more egalitarian distribution of wealth.

Piketty and other economists have proposed a solution to growing inequalities: a modest global wealth tax. He proposes that major economies cooperate to establish a global wealth tax that prevents the rich from shifting assets into other countries to avoid paying taxes in their own country. Even if there is a lack of global cooperation, Piketty suggests that large economies like the U.S. and European Union should create their own wealth and inheritance taxes to provide a temporary resolve from the dangers of the growing wealth gap.

Modeling Wealth Distributions: Related Work

In the past 30 years, the development of datasets from sources like the Survey of Consumer Finances allowed economists and mathematicians to quantify the degree of wealth distribution in the United States and abroad. Analysis of these surveys confirm that wealth is extremely concentrated in the U.S.: the top 1% hold one third of total wealth, the richest 5% hold more than half, and the bottom 40% hold little to no wealth at all.

The emergence of these datasets also allows economists to develop and refine models for understanding wealth distributions. A class of these models, called Asset-Exchange Models (AEMs) involve individual agents whose wealth changes due to binary transactions with other agents. An analogy to AEMs is kinetic theory, which describes molecules whose momentum and energy change due to interactions with other molecules. AEMs were first introduced in 1986 and provide idealized but useful methods to understanding wealth distributions. A recent paper by Jie Li, Bruce M.Boghosian, and Chengli Li demonstrates that, with appropriate features included, AEMs are capable of accurately modeling and explaining empirical economic data.

In this paper, we create an interactive version of the Extended Yard-Sale Model (EYSM), a variant of the Asset-Exchange Model first proposed in 2002 and refined in 2016 to include the idea of Wealth-Attained Average (WAA), which accounts for the privileges of the wealthy, like higher investment returns and lower interest rates. While many academic works have produced models that can replicate real-world data with remarkable accuracy, most of these models remain locked in an ivory tower, inaccessible and incomprehensible by a general audience. We aim to visualize an existing model in a way that is welcoming to the average user, allowing them to understand the factors that influence wealth distribution and see for themselves the effect of different policies on the wealth gap.

Methods

We used the Plotly Express library in Python to generate the interactive graphs above. The interactive simulations below were created using D3.js. The code snippet below shows the basic function that calculates individual transactions for all the simulations we present.

function transaction(event) { const wealthA = individuals[0].size; const wealthB = individuals[1].size; const a_pays = Math.random() < 0.5; let payment_amount = Math.min(wealthA, wealthB) * fracTransacted; if ((wealthA < wealthB) === a_pays) payment_amount *= (1 - fairness); draw_transfer(Math.round(payment_amount * (a_pays ? 1 : -1))) }

As you can see, the premise behind the wealth inequality model is encompassed in a relatively simple function.

We took an experimental approach to this paper, writing it in a format that would be accessible to academics, policymakers, and the general public. Using the interactive article format of Distill.pub, which is normally used for machine learning research, we embedded interactive visualizations into the paper that simulate the mathematical models used to predict wealth distribution outcomes. This retains a more academic format for economists and researchers, while allowing policymakers and general users to interact with and understand the model even without a mathematical or economic background. Our guiding values in the work were transparency and accessibility, which we retain by catering to a diverse range of audiences and reducing jargon as much as possible. Our hope is that this interactive paper format will help a broad table of stakeholders make better informed policy decisions when deciding how to reduce economic inequalities in society.

Our Interactive Model

In our interactive adaptation of the Extended Yard-Sale Model, we first present a free market closed economy without a wealth tax. To understand a single transaction between agents, consider two agents, Agent A and Agent B.

Agent A has some positive amount of wealth w and is transacting with Agent B, who has some positive amount of wealth \(x\). During this transaction, Agent A’s wealth is increased by \(\Delta w\), while Agent B’s wealth is decreased by \(\Delta w\), which can be positive or negative depending on \(\eta\) (representing a coin flip). $$ \Delta w = \gamma \cdot min(w, x)\eta $$

We model the Wealth-Attained Average, which simulates privilege, by biasing the coin flip. The bias is proportional to the difference between the two agents’ wealth normalized by the average wealth and multiplied by a WAA coefficient of the bias (\(\zeta)\), given by the expectation value of \(\eta\): $$E[\eta ] = \zeta \cdot \frac{w-x}{\frac{W}{N}}$$ where \(W\) is the total wealth \(w+x\), \(n\) = number of agents (2)

If the poorer agent is transferring to the wealthier agent, \(\Delta w\) is multiplied by a fairness coefficient of \(0 \leq \epsilon \leq 0.1\), representing charity and other social welfare programs that benefit the poor.

Discussion

We note several simplifications in our model that do not accurately represent the real world. First, we do not account for negative wealth in the version of the Yard Sale Model that we implement, so there is no representation of debt or loans given. Second, we present a highly simplified form of the wealth tax. In reality, the wealth tax would only apply to the top 1% wealth-holders in the population and would be implemented through taxes on inheritance, real estate, stock assets, and income in a far more nuanced way. Third, our indicator for "fairness" is exaggerated, as charity and welfare programs are voluntary and represent the willingness of people to donate their wealth, which is fairly unpredictable and difficult to represent in a single coefficient. While these simplifications seem to produce a highly elementary model of our true wealth dynamics, a paper that studies the Yard Sale Model, which is what our simulation is based on, finds that the model is in agreement with empirical data of an average error less than 0.16% over this time period.

Our visual design in this paper and embedded visualizations aims to communicate a clean, polished, and simple layout. We intentionally reduce the usage of colors to a simple black-and-white palette to reduce complexity for the viewer and presenting them with exactly the number of parameters that exist in the model. This emphasizes that the seemingly difficult mathematical equations presented in academic papers, which we include in the body of our text, can actually be understood through simple concepts. One guiding value in our work was transparency. The design of our visualization makes sure to include every single parameter we mention in the paper as a clearly marked slider that the user can control. We make sure to include every variable in the equations as a dynamic factor because we want to make it clear that there are no "hidden" features influencing the results behind the scenes: the user is fully able to change the model as they please. This makes the ultimate implications of the simulation as plausible and persuasive as possible.

The implications of our work suggest that a wealth tax or other wealth redistribution policy is crucial to preventing the exacerbation of already high socioeconomic inequalities. In every situation run in the simulation without a wealth tax--even with a low fraction transacted and a high level of "fairness"-- the wealth distribution inevitably concentrates wealth in the top 5% bracket of the agents. The moment a wealth tax is implemented, the distribution shifts. It does not remove the existence of economic class, but ensures that no tiny segment of the population holds more than 50% of the total wealth. Thus, even in our simple model, the wealth tax is a clear policy solution that makes far more impact than increasing social welfare and charity programs represented by the fairness coefficient.

Corroborating our findings, countries with strong state welfare programs, like the Nordic models of democratic-socialism, are still experiencing increasing inequalities and unsustainable wealth gaps. Without clear wealth redistribution policies, even welfare states (with high degrees of the fairness coefficient) succumb to a highly unequal wealth distribution. To combat this, we should further research wealth tax models, learning from the mistakes of European wealth taxes that were abolished in the last few decades. We must also realize that the system is inherently unfair: just like the agent in our transaction model is trapped in poverty if he starts with a low wealth, most of the world's poor have no escape. We should decrease the stigma around poverty and work to balance the extremes of the free market and state-run programs.

Future Work

In the future, this concept of interactive article simulations can be extended to more complex models, such as the Affine Wealth Model (AWM). This model expands on the Yard Sale Model by adding parameters that model negative wealth, a key factor missing from many wealth inequality simulators. In addition, models can incorporate policy solutions other than a uniform wealth tax, such as progressive income tax, the removal of inheritance tax loopholes, and bracketed wealth taxes. By giving users the ability to explore almost all policy solutions that currently exist, we allow the user themself to decide what is the most rational choice for their policy problem. The concept of this interactive paper could also be used in policy workshops, where experts in the field can use modular tools like the transaction simulator to understand the more complex equations presented in academic papers. A final vision for this work is similar to the EN-ROADS Climate Change Solutions Simulator, which is a transparent, freely-available policy simulation model that gives users the chance to design their own scenarios to limit future global warming. Like EN-ROADS, a wealth inequality simulator would allow the audience to try their own experiments and assumptions, and get immediate feedback on the likely impacts on wealth distribution. A key strength is that it would be able to run on an ordinary laptop with an internet connection in a fraction of a second, allowing it to be used real-time at in-person and virtual education events. Ultimately, this interactive simulator is a step towards making complicated and pressing economic problems more accessible to the public, improving our response to global inequalities.
